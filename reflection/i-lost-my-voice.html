I lost my voice
(It's okay. I have it back now).

Introduction

Long story short, I lost my voice. For six months, my voice flickered in and out, ever worsening. One day, it blew out. I could force out sounds—maybe a sentence a day if I tried—but it hurt too much to carry a conversation. Inflammation of my vocal cords, apparently. So for two months, I avoided speaking at all, waiting for medication to run its course. Although my ENT says my vocal cords now look normal, I'm still having trouble speaking; my voice is pitchy and hoarse. Frankly, I don't think I sound like me. And with 8 days to go before my internship starts, we'll see what happens.

This will be somewhat short: just two things. First, why it sucked so much—and the emotions that it carried. Then, more clinically, how I changed, and some general observations.

In principle, I'm the same person as before. I think the same way, I have the same opinions, and I want to contribute to conversations like I've always done. That's just not possible anymore. No—I grab my phone, pull up Notes, type a slurry of words with my two thumbs and pass it around. And all the while, everyone is just staring vacantly. They're not sure where to look. At each other? No, that might make me feel left out. But no use looking at me for eye contact—I'm busy typing. I type quickly, ignoring typos that can be guessed faster than corrected, and hope I'm not slowing the conversation down too much. Most times, I just don't comment.

Don't get me wrong: my friends are remarkably patient. They're not rushing me at all. All the same, I feel a sense of urgency; I feel like I'm wasting their time; I feel like I don't deserve their attention because I can't contribute equally.

These thoughts never truly fade. Every human interaction brings them back. Every workaround I have for not speaking—miming, writing notes, restructuring conversations into yes/no questions—is a deliberate reminder of my inability to speak. So too are the times I turn down invitations, decline to meet someone, or tell my friends I can't call them—but I'm happy to listen to them speak and text my responses back.

I may sound ungrateful—entitled, even—for not understanding my gift: that this is only temporary, and that others have it worse. I recognize that. But at the same time, could I not be entitled? Is that not fundamental to the human condition, to be able to speak?

Observations

The Small Stuff

Avoidance as Conflict Management

My conflict management strategy is avoidance. I order food via Ritual (a restaurant pre-order phone application). I frequent McDonald's more, because the location near me has a digital self-serve menu. I don't like holding up the line, and I don't like explaining that I can't talk. No, I avoid problems—and I need to be cognizant of this in the future.

I talk. A lot.

I took a trip to Waterloo with a friend, and we met up with high school friends. Even after two months of not talking, I found myself wanting to add to the conversation or comment at a high frequency. That's not necessarily bad, but something to remember—am I always adding value?

The Big Stuff

Identity as Presentation

I attach my identity to my voice. I know it's not rational, but I do it anyway. After all, it's how I primarily communicate. And if I can't express my thoughts or feelings, what did it matter that I was still otherwise me? Did I still have an identity without my voice?

I conducted a (poorly designed) experiment: I asked a few people for five words that described me. Here are the responses:

Intelligent, analytical, logical, "slightly stubborn", "both results oriented and process oriented. It's an interesting mix"

The interesting thing is that there is no mention of my voice or speech. Granted, I don't have one of those silky-smooth, made-for-radio voices. But at some point, all five of these survey respondents had told me I was articulate. (You may have noticed that hasn't translated into my writing.) Yet when pressed for words that best described me, articulate never came up. Whether for a classmate or personal confidante, the qualities that most stood out about me had nothing to do with my voice. And certainly, inability to speak impedes my ability to show that I'm perceptive and or comforting. But a lot of qualities—intelligent, analytical, hard-working—are intrinsic. This is important for me to remember—what others see as my best traits continue to exist despite my temporary mutism. Moving forward, I will demonstrate these qualities as best I can.

Accessibility is a Tough Challenge

When I first stopped talking, it was hard to adjust. How do you tell your waiter that you can't talk? Can you order without pointing at the menu? That seems crass. And where do you go to ask these questions? I found myself lost, and unable to communicate with the world around me.

To be fair, mutism cannot be managed by regulation or accessibility measures, because it's not a structural problem. It's not the same as building ramps or motorized doors or including audio jacks at ATM's—not that these solutions are perfect either. But mutism—especially when temporary—is a unique type of problem. Solutions must come from empowering the mute to speak.

And sign language isn't good enough. It carries all the overhead of a new language, requiring users to learn an entirely new vocabulary and syntax. Even then, it only works if both parties understand sign language—often, that can be isolating. Maybe there's the solution: mandatory ASL-as-a-second-language high school classes. But as a Canadian who was taught French in public school, I wouldn't count on education reform to solve this problem.

It's a weird feeling, on my end, to dissect a problem without the slightest notion of a solution. But on this one, I'm stumped. I made a little web app to help me type faster and communicate with others via phone, but it's a poor workaround. I'll put a pin in it for now; maybe one day I'll have the right tools to tackle this.
